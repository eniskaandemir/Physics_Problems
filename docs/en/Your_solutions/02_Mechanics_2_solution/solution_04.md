# Task 04 – Energy and Momentum

## Problem Statement

A $0.5$ kg block slides down a frictionless track from a height of $3.0$ m. At the bottom, it collides and sticks to a $1.5$ kg block initially at rest.

Determine the speed of the combined mass immediately after the collision.

## Theory

This problem has two stages.

### Stage 1 – Descent on a Frictionless Track

Since the track is frictionless, mechanical energy is conserved:

$$
mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2
$$

This gives the speed just before collision.

### Stage 2 – Perfectly Inelastic Collision

Because the two blocks stick together, momentum is conserved during the collision:

$$
m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v_f
$$

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Speed of the First Block at the Bottom

The mass of the moving block is

$$
m_1 = 0.5 \text{ kg}
$$

The height is

$$
h = 3.0 \text{ m}
$$

Using energy conservation,

$$
mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2
$$

Cancel the mass:

$$
gh = \frac{1}{2}v^2
$$

Thus,

$$
v = \sqrt{2gh}
$$

Substitute $g = 9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$:

$$
v = \sqrt{2 \cdot 9.81 \cdot 3.0}
$$

$$
v = \sqrt{58.86}
$$

$$
v \approx 7.67 \text{ m/s}
$$

So just before collision,

$$
v_1 \approx 7.67 \text{ m/s}
$$

### 2. Apply Momentum Conservation

The second block has mass

$$
m_2 = 1.5 \text{ kg}
$$

and is initially at rest, so

$$
v_2 = 0
$$

Using momentum conservation:

$$
m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v_f
$$

Substitute values:

$$
0.5 \cdot 7.67 + 1.5 \cdot 0 = (0.5 + 1.5)v_f
$$

$$
3.835 = 2.0 v_f
$$

$$
v_f = \frac{3.835}{2.0}
$$

$$
v_f \approx 1.92 \text{ m/s}
$$

## Final Result

The speed of the combined mass just after the collision is

$$
v_f \approx 1.92 \text{ m/s}
$$

## Interpretation

Before the collision, gravitational potential energy is converted into kinetic energy. During the collision, momentum is conserved, but because the collision is perfectly inelastic, part of the kinetic energy is transformed into internal energy such as heat, deformation, or sound.
